Stepping out of your comfort zone is one thing, but moving to a whole new country is another level of "learning on the fly." After some time settling in, I’ve realized that study abroad life is a mix of high-pressure academics and unexpected cultural quirks.

## The Academic Reality Check
If you think the only struggle is the language, think again. While some professors have accents that are... let's say "challenging" to decipher, the real hurdle is the system itself.

- **Fixed and Fast:** Everything here is on a strict rails. From the moment you sign up for a course, your exam dates are set in stone. There is no guesswork and definitely no room for procrastination.

- **Denser Material:** The curriculum feels much heavier than what I was used to in Vietnam. Semesters are shorter, and the volume of knowledge packed into a few months is intense.

- **The Resource Gap:** For foundational subjects like Math, it’s quite tough. Unlike back home, there aren't many "senior secrets" or collections of past exam papers to practice with. You really have to master the core concepts from scratch.

## Research Life: Familiar Faces, Different Places
Doing research here feels like a premium version of what I did in Vietnam. The professors are just as passionate and supportive, but the environment has some notable upgrades.

<img src="/assets/images/japan/campus.jpg" alt="Campus" width="100%" />

- **The Lab Environment:** My workspace is great. I have a wide desk and a view right by the window. Having that natural light makes the long hours of data crunching much more bearable.

- **The Publishing Loop:** There is a specific workflow for publishing papers here. Beyond just writing and self-editing, there is a mandatory third-party proofreading step. To be honest, I haven't seen it make a huge difference yet, but it’s part of the ritual.

- **Financial Freedom:** One thing I really appreciate is that the university covers all expenses for attending conferences. It’s a huge relief to focus on the science without worrying about the bill.

## Exploring the Map
Japan isn't just one big neon city. Every place I've visited has its own personality.

- **Kanazawa:** This is my local area, and I have to say, Kanazawa Castle and the Kenrokuen Garden are stunning. In my opinion, they are even more beautiful than the famous spots in Osaka.
<img src="/assets/images/japan/kanazawa.jpg" alt="Kanazawa" width="100%" />

- **Kyoto:** Honestly, it didn't leave a huge impression on me. It felt like a never-ending cycle of temples.
<img src="/assets/images/japan/kyoto.jpg" alt="Kyoto" width="100%" />

- **Osaka:** Vibrant, crowded, and full of life. I ran into so many fellow Vietnamese people there! The vibe is much more relaxed than in Ishikawa, but the trade-off is that the streets are quite dirty. Seeing trash on the ground was a bit of a shock given Japan's reputation for cleanliness.
<img src="/assets/images/japan/osaka.jpg" alt="Osaka" width="100%" />

## Food and Festivities
Living here means my stomach is constantly on a journey of its own.

- **The MVP:** My favorite food so far? The shrimp tempura at Sushiro. It’s simple, but I love it.

- **The Basics:** I've tried the local Udon and Ramen. The Udon broth suits my taste well, but overall, they haven't blown me away yet.

- **The Grocery Paradox:** Buying fresh, raw ingredients is surprisingly cheap here. However, a bag of rice is incredibly expensive. It’s a strange balance for a student budget!

- **Holidays:** Japan has public holidays almost every month, but they are very scattered. Since I live in a more quiet, less populated region, I haven't really seen any big traditional festivals yet. My days off are usually just peaceful breaks from the lab.

Going abroad is a lesson in patience and observation. It’s about finding the beauty in a quiet garden and the humor in a confusing lecture.
